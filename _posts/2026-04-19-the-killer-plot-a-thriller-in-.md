In a world where the lines between reality and fiction blur, Bob Odenkirk's thriller attempts to entice with its dark and twisted tale of murder, mystery, and deceit. The story unfolds like a twisted puzzle, with pieces that don't quite fit together seamlessly. The protagonist, a former hitman turned amateur detective, stumbles upon a web of secrets that lead him down a rabbit hole of corruption and deceit. But as the investigation unfolds, the true killer remains elusive, leaving the audience to piece together the evidence.

The film's greatest strength lies in its atmospheric setting, a creepy mansion that exudes an air of foreboding. The cinematography is striking, capturing the eerie glow of the dimly lit rooms and the faint scent of decay. However, the editing is where the thriller falters, skipping crucial moments and cutting away too soon. The pacing is uneven, with long stretches of silence punctuated by shallow character interactions.

Despite these flaws, Odenkirk's signature wit and charm shine through, making the film's most memorable moments all the more delightful. The supporting cast, including Taylor Kitsch and Michael McKean, bring their A-game, but even their talents can't salvage the weak storytelling. Ultimately, "The Killer Plot" feels like a promising idea that got lost in the editing room, leaving the audience to ponder the mystery, but ultimately, to be left wondering.
