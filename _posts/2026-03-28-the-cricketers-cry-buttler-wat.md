In the lush Eden Gardens, where bats and balls entwine like lovers in a tender pas de deux, a new discord echoes through the air. The mighty Kolkata Knight Riders, once the undisputed champions of the Indian Premier League, now succumb to the whispers of dissent. Two stalwarts of the game, Jos Buttler and Shane Watson, have joined the chorus of critics, their voices a clarion call to reform the very fabric of the IPL.

Their words are like a clarion call to arms, a rallying cry that echoes through the stands, striking fear into the hearts of those who think themselves immune to change. "Cricket has been 11-a-side for so long," they cry, a lamentation of a game that has lost its way, a symphony of skill and luck turned into a cacophony of chaos. The players, once the masters of their craft, now stumble through the game, their techniques honed to perfection but their tactics rudimentary. It's a game that has lost its art, its soul, its very essence. The impact players, those 11 players who can change the course of the game, are the relics of a bygone era, their presence a reminder of what could have been.

Buttler and Watson are not alone in their criticism. The likes of MS Dhoni, the master strategist, and the wily Abhinav Mukund, the master of the middle order, are also speaking out, their voices a plea for change, for a return to the days of skill and strategy. The game is at a crossroads, its future uncertain. Will it ever return to its glory days, or will it succumb to the pressures of the modern game? Only time will tell.
