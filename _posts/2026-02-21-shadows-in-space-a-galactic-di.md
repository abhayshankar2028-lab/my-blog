As I sat in the dimly lit cinema, the eerie glow of the screen cast an otherworldly aura, beckoning me to enter the cosmos. "Good Luck, Have Fun, Don't Die" promised to be an adventure, but little did I know, it was a cosmic catastrophe waiting to unfold. Sam Rockwell, the charismatic lead, was lost in a sea of sci-fi clich√©s, his rugged charm unable to save the film from its mundane depths.

Like a spacecraft hurtling through the void, the story careened from one predictable plot twist to the next, its attempts at complexity falling flat. The special effects, touted as cutting-edge, looked more like a sloppy DIY job, with characters vanishing into thin air like ghosts. The script, a jumbled mess of techno-speak and sci-fi jargon, left me scratching my head, struggling to make sense of the nonsensical world it presented.

As the credits rolled, I felt a sense of disappointment wash over me, like dust settling on a neglected spaceship. "Good Luck, Have Fun, Don't Die" was a sci-fi mess, a disappointment that left me wondering if the film's creators had ever set foot in the cosmos.
